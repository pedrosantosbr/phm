II The Problem

Modern medicine is
data-rich and
intelligence-poor.

An average inpatient stay generates more than 80,000 discrete data points. Most are read by no one. The few that are read are read late. Care is delivered by exhausted humans operating across systems that were never designed to talk to each other.

— Source: Vellum 2025 internal benchmark across 14 academic & community sites; Tier-1 EHR systems; n=2.1M encounters.
  1. i.

    Information without intelligence.

    Clinicians spend 49% of their time on documentation and chart review — most of it past tense. The signal lives in the present, but the system only describes the past.

    tag · cognitive-load · attention
  2. ii.

    Risk found in retrospect.

    Sepsis, deterioration, and readmission patterns are well understood — usually after the event. The window for intervention closes before pattern recognition catches up.

    tag · latency · decompensation · M&M
  3. iii.

    Workflows held together by hand.

    Care coordination still depends on pages, sticky notes, and shift handoffs. Critical context is lost in translation between teams, systems, and units.

    tag · coordination · handoff · sbar
  4. iv.

    Compliance as a tax, not a feature.

    Quality measures and regulatory reporting consume hours of clinical time, often through retrofitted dashboards nobody trusts and everybody must use.

    tag · cms · joint-commission · qa

Everything below — the names considered, the palette, the type, the photographic direction — was selected to communicate one thing: clinical authority delivered with human warmth.

01 / chosen

Vellum

From the parchment on which medicine first inscribed itself. Short, abstract, archival. Two syllables, soft consonants, owns its category.

02 / alternate

Kairos

Greek: the right moment. The opportune intervention. Carries the temporal precision Vellum's monitoring product is built around.

03 / alternate

Lumen

A unit of light, and the inside of a vessel. A double medical resonance: visibility and circulation. Slightly more clinical, slightly less editorial.
